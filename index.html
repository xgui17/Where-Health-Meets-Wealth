üëº The More Education, the Fewer Angel Babies

    Figure 1 depicts the relationship between average years that people aged 25+ spend in formal education and average infant mortality rates for over 180 countries, and it reveals a negative correlation. Developed countries predominantly occupy the end with higher levels of average schooling and lower infant mortality rates, while developing countries are clustered at the other end.

    This visualization emphasizes that developed countries tend to invest more in education systems and support more educational opportunities. It also underscores the significance of education as a Social Determinant of Health in improving health outcomes of infants and mitigating risks to newborns. This insight may help policy makers to prioritize educational investments and programs that may contribute to improved infant health and overall population well-being.

üíâ Wealth's Protective Embrace of Infants

    The choropleth map (Figure 2) displays the average vaccination rates among 1-year-olds for several vaccines, including Hepatitis B (HepB3), Measles (MCV1), Polio (Pol3), and Diphtheria tetanus toxoid and pertussis (DTP3). The lighter coloration observed in African countries indicates lower vaccine coverage, which may reflect weaker healthcare systems and insufficient health funding.

    This visualization draws attention to the disparities in access to vaccinations and more broadly to basic healthcare services. It highlights the urgent need for targeted interventions, improved healthcare infrastructure, and increased funding in regions with lower vaccination coverage. Addressing these gaps can lead to higher immunization rates, disease prevention, and enhanced overall health outcomes.

üí∏ Pathways of Health Funding

    Figure 3 illustrates the flow of Development Assistance for Health (DAH) funding between different regions from 1990 to 2021. The width of the links represents the amount of funding flow, while the size of the nodes corresponds to the average GDP per capita of the region. Major donors, such as North America and the EU, are characterized by higher GDP per capita and exhibit significant contributions to DAH funding, while major recipients like Asia and Africa tend to have lower GDP per capita and received much more DAH fundings than donated.

    This visualization highlights the economic disparities and regional imbalances in healthcare financing. Policymakers can leverage this information to promote equitable distribution of DAH funding, encourage collaboration among regions, and foster sustainable investments in healthcare infrastructure. By addressing funding gaps and promoting fair resource allocation, global health inequities can be addressed more effectively.

Data Preprocessing

    The data sources this project include Kaggle, IHME (Institution for Health Metrics and Evaluation), and geojson-maps.

    The first dataset contains relevant health, economic and demographic information. There are multiple rows for each country, which are data collected in different years. Only the most recent data are kept. An average infant vaccination rate is obatined by calculating the mean of four attributes which are the average infant vaccine coverage rates of Hepatitis B (HepB3), Measles (MCV1), Polio (Pol3), and Diphtheria tetanus toxoid and pertussis (DTP3) respectively. 3-digit ISO code are merged to the dataset for mapping convenience, and necessary string replacements are made to clean up typos and unify country or dependency name. Attributes that are not needed in this project are dropped. Figure 1 and 2 utilized information from this dataset. The regional average GDP in figure 3 is also obtained from this dataset.

    The second dataset contains information on Development Assistance for Health (DAH) fundings from 1990 to 2021. Fundings with unknown donor or recipient country, which is denoted by ‚ÄúUnallocated/Unspecified‚Äù, are dropped. Fundings used for administration purposes or meant for global use are also dropped. Necessary string replacements are made to clean up typos and unify country or dependency name. Regional information are added to the dataset in alignment to the previous dataset. The remaining fundings are then aggregated by region to calculate total donated and received fundings. Figure 3 utilized this dataset.

    The third dataset is a json file containing demographical information of the globe. It is used in figure 2 together with the first dataset with correspondence on the ISO code.
